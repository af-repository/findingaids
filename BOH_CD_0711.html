Collection Inventory

Disease Reports, 1970-2006 

Historical note

The Board of Health is required to report occurrences and outbreaks of certain communicable diseases to the MA Bureau of Infectious Disease Prevention, formerly the Bureau of Communicable Disease Control, a division of the MA Department of Public Health that works to improve quality of life through the elimination of communicable diseases. Reports and communications are also sent between Somerville and other cities and towns to notify hospitals, clinics, and health departments of contagious disease cases.

Scope and Contents

This series contains disease reports and correspondence sent between the Somerville Board of Health and state and city health agencies concerning Somerville residents and patients of Somerville physicians, hospitals, and medical clinics.

Related Material note

There are additional disease reports in the Board of Health Laurence Howard, Executive Clerk series.

Disease and Dog Bite Notifications, 1970-1973 

Scope and Contents

Contains Somerville resident disease and dog bite injury reports in the form of cards and correspondence between the Somerville Board of Health, the Massachusetts Department of Public Health, the Somerville Hospital, Massachusetts General Hospital, the Boston Board of Health, the Cambridge Health Department, and nearby towns. There are also monthly reports of venereal diseases from Mass. Department of Public Health to the Somerville Board of Health and monthly contagious disease reports from the East Somerville Health Center. Cards include information such as patient name, age, disease or dog bite injury, address, physician, school attendance, milk vendor, and additional dog bite reporting.

Arrangement

Records are arranged chronologically.

Somerville Case Files, 1984-2006 

Scope and Contents

Contains patient case reports and correspondence pertaining to occurrences and outbreaks of communicable diseases in Somerville as reported to the MA Department of Public Health. Reported diseases include bacterial and parasitic gastroenteritis (caused by E-coli, listeria, salmonella, etc.), Hepatitis (A, B, and C), Legionnaires’ disease, Lyme disease, Malaria, Meningitis, Pertussis (whooping cough), Rubella (German measles), toxoplasmosis, Typhoid Fever, and Varicella (chickenpox). Hepatitis case files include correspondence, immunization records, and case reports from an outbreak of Hepatitis A in the Mystic Housing Project development from July 1984 to January 1985. The correspondence includes notice of a damages claim filed by tenants against the City of Somerville, the Board of Health, and the Somerville Housing Authority.

Related Material note

Box #667 contains legal case files pertaining to the damages claim filed by Mystic Housing development tenants against the City of Somerville.

Arrangement

Records are arranged in two sections: 1. Case Files, 1981-2003 (Alphabetical by disease), 2. Case Files, 1992-2006 (Chronological).

Somerville Contagious Hospital Records, 1914-1947 

Historical note

The Somerville Contagious Hospital was established in 1905 in an existing building located at 1323 Broadway at the corner of North St. in West Somerville. In 1910 the City built a tuberculosis ward (colloquially called the Somerville Tuberculosis Hospital) on land adjacent to the Hospital. The Hospital served as an isolation hospital, admitting patients with communicable diseases including tuberculosis, scarlet fever, and diphtheria from Somerville and other towns and cities such as Arlington, Cambridge, Everett, Malden, Medford, Melrose, Norwood, Winchester, and Woburn. In 1912 the Contagious Hospital had the capacity for 35 scarlet fever patients, 25 diphtheria patients, and 24 tuberculosis patients. Over time the Hospital grew to include treatment for diseases such as erysipelas, measles, mumps, pneumonia, strep throat, and whooping cough (pertussis).

“Monthly Bulletin of the State Board of Health of Massachusetts” 7, no. 2 (February 1912). Boston: Wright & Potter.

Scope and Contents

This series contains records of patient admission, condition, care, discharge, and fees produced by the Somerville Contagious Hospital.

Arrangement

All sections are arranged chronologically.

Related Material note

Tuberculosis Records, 1903-2013 

Scope and Contents

Contains case files, patient information cards, financial correspondence, publications and disease reports concerning active and latent cases of tuberculosis.

Case Files, 1903-2013 

Scope and Contents

Early case files (1903-1960) include Nurse’s cards and notes on patient condition, family, living conditions, and institutional admittance and discharge, as well as reports and correspondence sent between the Board of Health, medical institutions, state agencies, and local town and city health agencies concerning patient cases. Later case files contain test results, clinic notes, and MA Department of Public Health forms recording latent and active tuberculosis cases.

Correspondence, 1913-1937 

Scope and Contents

Contains correspondence between the Board of Health and the MA Department of Public Health, the MA Department of Public Welfare, and the State Trustees of Hospitals for Consumptives concerning reimbursements for care of patients with tuberculosis.

Nurse's Cards, 1928-1942, 1966-1968 

Scope and Contents

Contains medical record cards of deceased patients. Cards include information such as patient name, address, birthplace and nationality, age, occupation, employer, home condition, institutional history, and extensive notes on patient condition and institutional admittance and discharge.

Arrangement

Cards are arranged by the year of patient death and alphabetically by surname within each year.

Patient Cards, circa 1912-1973 

Scope and Contents

Contains cards with patient information such as name, address, age, sex, nationality, family information, home conditions, type of tuberculosis, doctor, sanatorium or hospital admittance and discharge dates, and condition upon discharge.

Arrangement

Cards are arranged alphabetically by patient surname in two groupings.
